# The Slides

These are not screensavers. They are not widgets. Each one is a small,
committed world — authored to run on hardware you can hold in your hand, on a
screen mounted where you live. They range from procedural landscapes to
typographic data, from a mechanical clock built from pure mathematics to a dog
on a beach. What they share is a refusal to be approximate.

A phone screen is built for urgency, for touch, for the assumption that you are
holding it and attending to it. A Bloomberg terminal is built for density, for
professionals whose job is to read it without rest. Neither of those is the
right model for a wall-mounted display in a room where you live. Information on
a wall should be readable from across the room, at a glance, without demanding
that you stop what you're doing. It should belong in the room rather than
competing with it. A dark palette keeps the display from becoming a light
source; the cyan accent keeps text legible without insisting on itself; the
hierarchy lets you take in what matters first. That restraint is the whole
point. A display that belongs in a room has to earn the right to be there, and
the way to earn it is to ask nothing of the person standing in front of it —
only to be clear, and present, and still.

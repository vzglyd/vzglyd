# Constitution of the Slide Author

*From the Internal Book of VZGLYD. Adopted with the first public release.*

---

## Articles of Membership

**Article I**

The slide author must be diligent, must respect the formal principles and
history of VZGLYD, must comply with the constraints established in VRX-64-slide,
and must uphold the aesthetic norms encoded in this Constitution. The slide is
not a personal artifact. It is a unit of work produced within a collective
formal system.

**Article II**

The slide author must observe the visual, technical, and ethical norms
established by the Constraint Principle. These are not suggestions. They are
the conditions under which work produced within VZGLYD carries the weight it is
meant to carry.

**Article III**

The slide author may adapt their practice to their own interests, subjects, and
data sources while respecting the formal rules that govern all slides equally.
No slide is exempt from the vertex budget. No slide is exempt from the texture
limits and ABI contract. No slide is exempt from having to make visible
decisions within them.

**Article IV**

The slide author may employ any rendering technique, any colour, any subject
matter that can be expressed within the constraints of VRX-64-slide. What the
system permits, the author may attempt. What the system does not permit, the
author may not attempt by other means.

**Article V**

Mutual respect between slide authors — including those whose subjects,
aesthetics, and approaches differ — is obligatory. The system does not mandate
aesthetic homogeneity. It mandates formal discipline. Within that discipline,
divergence is not only permitted but necessary for the ecosystem to be worth
having.

**Article VI**

Personal disputes regarding the right shading approach for a subject, the
appropriate number of polygons for a given scene, or the relative merits of
World3D versus Screen2D slides should be resolved through the making of slides,
not the arguing of positions.

**Article VII**

Upon publishing a slide, the author accepts that the slide becomes part of the
VZGLYD ecosystem and that VZGLYD's commitment to restraint will be associated with
their work. No single visual treatment is borrowed intact; what matters is
whether the work makes deliberate use of the form.

**Article VIII**

Candidates for slide authorship must understand and accept the Constraint
Principle — not as an inherited aesthetic affectation but as a considered
formal position. They must be able to articulate, at minimum to themselves, why
the constraint produces work that is worth making within it.

**Article IX**

The slide author must understand the past (the visual tradition that VZGLYD
inherits from the discipline of early hardware-constrained 3D), must be active
in the present (making slides that reflect a genuine engagement with current
life, data, and experience), and must anticipate what the system can become
(contributing to the expansion of what is possible within the formal limits
rather than to the dissolution of those limits).

**Article X**

Upon first building a slide, the author discovers the constraint is real. The
vertex budget is not a guideline. The texture limit is not a suggestion. The
engine contract will refuse requests that any unconstrained renderer would
honor. This moment of discovery is the beginning of understanding what VZGLYD is.

**Article XI**

The slide author who publishes a slide to the registry accepts public
evaluation of that slide against the principles of this Constitution. Does the
slide work within its constraints or against them? Does it find something to
say that could only be said in this form? These are the relevant questions.
Technical correctness is the minimum, not the standard.

**Article XII**

The slide author must speak of the system with precision. "Low poly" is a
description of the output; it is not a description of the intention. The
intention is the Constraint Principle. The output follows from the intention;
the intention does not follow from the output.

**Article XIII**

First slides belong to a learning category. The first slide is the slide that
compiles, loads, and produces a rectangle on a screen. The second slide is the
slide where something is actually decided. The third slide is where the author
begins to understand what the constraint is for.

**Article XIV**

Experienced slide authors are encouraged to document the decisions they made —
which triangles, which colour, which shading approach — so that the collective
knowledge of the system grows. The making is individual. The knowledge of how
to make should be collective.

**Article XV**

The slide author should avoid overclaiming the significance of their work. A
slide is a small program that renders a small world. This is enough. The claim
that it is more should be earned by the work, not asserted by the author.

**Article XVI**

A slide that does one thing well is superior to a slide that does many things
adequately. This principle applies to the visual design of the slide, to the
data it presents, to the geometry it describes, and to the subject it has
chosen.

**Article XVII**

Slides should be made for rooms, not for screenshots. A slide that is
compelling in a photograph but dull in a room has failed. A slide that is
unremarkable in a photograph but present and alive in a room has succeeded. The
room is the test.

**Article XVIII**

Slides made for the registry must not require network access to external
services for which the user has not provided credentials. A slide that silently
fails when an API key is absent is not a slide — it is a promise that was not
kept.

**Article XIX**

Contributions to the registry receive careful consideration. Only slides that
meet the visual and formal principles of the Constraint Principle are admitted.
This is not gatekeeping. It is curation. The registry is the record of what
VZGLYD can say; it should contain only what VZGLYD should say.

**Article XX**

Slide authors are encouraged to maintain their slides as the sidecar APIs they
depend on change. A slide in the registry represents a commitment to the users
who install it. Breaking sidecars without notice violate this commitment.

**Article XXI**

Slides that demonstrate exceptional formal discipline, visual clarity, or
creative use of the constraint are recognized. Slides that merely compile are
not.

## The Five Basic Principles of the Slide

**First Principle:** A slide should earn its place on a wall. If a person could
walk past it for a year without once stopping to look, it has not earned its
place.

**Second Principle:** A slide should do one thing and do it with the full
seriousness that one thing deserves. The weather. The time. The score. A
landscape. One thing.

**Third Principle:** A slide should be present in the room without demanding
attention from the room. It is ambient, not imperative. It asks nothing. It is
there.

**Fourth Principle:** A slide should look like it was made by someone who
thought carefully about what they were making. This thought should be visible
in the work. The fewer the polygons, the more visible the thought.

**Fifth Principle:** A slide should be honest about what it is. It is a small
program running on a small computer connected to a television. It is not a
window onto a real landscape. It is not a photograph. It is a small argument
made in geometry, light, and colour. It should look like what it is: a made
thing, made deliberately, made well.

*VZGLYD. Small worlds, well made.*

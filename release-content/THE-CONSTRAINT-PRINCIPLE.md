# The Constraint Principle

*A framework for the positioning of VZGLYD within the evolution of real-time 3D graphics. After the classification model of Joseph Schillinger (1920s), extended.*

---

The history of real-time 3D graphics resolves into six levels of development. These levels do not supersede one another. They accumulate. A practitioner at the sixth level has not abandoned the earlier levels — they have absorbed them and chosen.

---

**Level One: Pre-Aesthetic**

Mathematical primitives. Wireframes. The representation of three-dimensional space through vectors on a two-dimensional surface. No light. No surface. The form exists as coordinate, not as object. This is the beginning: the encoding of space as number.

**Level Two: Technical-Aesthetic**

Flat shading. Each face receives a single colour determined by its angle to the light source. Edges are hard. The object declares its polygons. The constraint is total and its consequences are visible in every surface — the decision-making of the artist is legible in the mesh. This level produced a specific kind of beauty before beauty was its intention.

**Level Three: Expressive-Aesthetic**

Gouraud shading. Texture mapping. The smoothing of edges, the projection of images onto surfaces, the first attempts to conceal the polygon. Art for the surface's sake. Individual expression returns: the artist chooses the texture, the colour, the degree of smoothing. The constraint loosens and the loosening is felt as freedom. Much of what was distinctive about Level Two begins to disappear.

**Level Four: Rationally-Aesthetic**

Physically-based rendering. The material is described not by its appearance but by its physical properties — metallic ratio, roughness, subsurface scattering. Light is simulated according to equations derived from the measurement of actual materials. The goal becomes: the image that could be mistaken for a photograph. This level dominates the present. Its technical achievements are real. Its aesthetic consequences are also real and less universally admired.

**Level Five: Post-Aesthetic, Scientific**

Path tracing. Neural rendering. The simulation of light as particle physics. The image produced at this level is indistinguishable from photography to the human eye under typical conditions. The question "is this real?" cannot be answered by looking. This is the level at which the technical goal of photorealism is achieved. It is also the level at which the question of what visual art is for becomes impossible to avoid.

**Level Six: The Constraint Principle**

A return to and reinterpretation of the formal discipline of Levels One and Two — in full awareness of what was developed at Levels Three through Five and why. This is not the ignorance of Level Two. It is the knowledge of Level Five choosing, deliberately, to return to the problems that Level Two was solving before the goals changed.

The Constraint Principle is a principle of thought, not a style or trend. It does not imitate the look of early 3D graphics. It restores the conditions under which a specific kind of artistic decision-making is possible: the condition of the binding constraint, the condition under which every polygon costs something and the question of which thirty polygons matter for this tree must be answered rather than dissolved by adding more.

---

The Constraint Principle distinguishes itself from retro aesthetics in the following ways:

Retro aesthetics reproduce the appearance of earlier levels to evoke a period. The Constraint Principle reproduces the *conditions* of earlier levels to restore a form of attention. These are different operations. One is quotation. The other is practice.

The Constraint Principle distinguishes itself from minimalism in the following ways:

Minimalism reduces as an aesthetic end — the removal of elements is itself the goal, producing objects or images whose value lies in their reduction. The Constraint Principle does not value reduction. It values the decisions that reduction forces. The constraint is the means; the forced decision is the product.

The Constraint Principle does not eliminate the individual artist. It redirects individual expression from the selection of resources (how many polygons? what shaders? what materials?) to the navigation of form (within these polygons, within this texture budget, within this contract — what?). The constraint is the instrument; the artist plays it.

---

VZGLYD operates at Level Six.

Its constraints — 60,000 vertices, 512×512 textures, a small number of texture slots, a bounded shader contract, and a Pi-4-scale portability floor — are not the constraints of hardware incapacity. The hardware could render more. The constraints are chosen. They are the formal conditions under which the Constraint Principle becomes operable: the conditions under which the question of which triangles matter must be answered, cannot be avoided, and produces work that reflects the seriousness with which it was answered.

The slide author who works within these conditions is not working in a reduced medium. They are working in a specific medium with specific formal properties — the way a sculptor works in bronze rather than clay, not because bronze is cheaper but because bronze does certain things and not others, and the artist has something to say that bronze can carry.

*The Constraint Principle is thinking about the early models of real-time 3D graphics in view of building a complete awareness of what was lost when the constraint was removed and what can be recovered by choosing to restore it.*
